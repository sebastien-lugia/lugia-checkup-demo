# Reference Note — What is a Work System Framework (WSF)?

*Based on research by Steven Alter, University of San Francisco (2013–2025)*

---

## 1. Overview

A **Work System Framework (WSF)** is a conceptual tool developed within **Work System Theory (WST)** — an academic framework in the Information Systems (IS) discipline — for describing, analyzing, and designing systems in organizational settings. It is one of three core components of WST, alongside the **definition of a work system** and the **Work System Life Cycle Model (WSLC)**.

The WSF provides a structured lens for understanding how IT-enabled systems operate in organizations, with the goal of making systems analysis accessible to both business professionals and IS/IT specialists.

---

## 2. What is a Work System?

Before describing the framework itself, it is essential to understand what a *work system* is:

> **"A system in which human participants and/or machines perform work (processes and activities) using information, technology, and other resources to produce specific product/services for internal and/or external customers and/or for themselves."**

A work system may be:

- **Sociotechnical** — involving human participants who perform at least part of the work alongside technology.
- **Totally automated** — where machines perform all of the work (e.g., search engines, embedded control systems).

Importantly, **an information system (IS) is a special type of work system** — one whose essential activities are devoted to capturing, transmitting, storing, retrieving, deleting, manipulating, and/or displaying information. All IS properties are therefore inherited from the broader category of work system.

---

## 3. The Work System Framework — Core Elements

The WSF identifies the key elements that constitute any work system. These elements are grouped into:

### Elements *within* the Work System
| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Processes and Activities** | The work performed — may be structured (highly automated) or unstructured (creative, judgment-based). Activities may be performed by humans, machines, or both. |
| **Participants** | The human actors involved in performing activities (employees, contractors, etc.). |
| **Information** | Any evidence or knowledge — utterances, records, evaluations, data — used in performing work. Not limited to computer-stored data. |
| **Technology** | Tools, software, hardware, and other technical resources used to support activities. |

### Elements *surrounding* the Work System

Certains de ces éléments sont partiellement internes, partiellement externes ; d'autres sont considérés comme largement extérieurs au système tout en ayant des effets directs sur son fonctionnement.

| Element | Position | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Customers** | Partiellement interne/externe | Destinataires internes ou externes des produits/services du système. Les clients participent souvent aux activités au sein même du système (ex. : un patient lors d'un examen médical). |
| **Product/Services** | Partiellement interne/externe | Ce que le système produit ou fournit. Le terme combiné évite les débats sur la distinction produits/services. |
| **Infrastructure** | Largement externe | Ressources humaines, informationnelles et techniques *partagées* par plusieurs systèmes de travail (ex. : une direction informatique mutualisée). Non associée exclusivement à un seul système. |
| **Environment** | Largement externe | Le contexte organisationnel, concurrentiel, réglementaire, culturel, technique et démographique pertinent pour le système. |
| **Strategies** | Largement externe | Les stratégies pouvant exister à trois niveaux : entreprise, département, et système de travail. Elles influencent directement les choix de conception et d'exploitation du système, même si elles sont formulées en dehors de lui. |

> **Note importante** : Le WSF compte donc **9 éléments** au total. Les 4 premiers (Processus & Activités, Participants, Information, Technologies) sont *entièrement internes* au système. Clients et Produits/Services sont *à cheval* entre l'intérieur et l'extérieur. Infrastructure, Environnement et Stratégies sont *largement extérieurs* au système, tout en exerçant sur lui des effets directs.

Le cadre intègre délibérément les dimensions techniques et humaines, reflétant la nature sociotechnique de la plupart des systèmes réels.

## 4. The Work System Life Cycle Model (WSLC)

The WSLC complements the WSF by explaining *how work systems change over time*. It identifies four phases:

1. **Initiation** — Recognition that a change is needed.
2. **Development** — Design, acquisition, or configuration of resources.
3. **Implementation** — Installation and transition to the new system.
4. **Operation and Maintenance** — Ongoing use, adaptation, and upkeep.

Crucially, WST distinguishes between:
- **Planned change** — through formal projects progressing through the phases above.
- **Unplanned change** — through workarounds, participant adaptations, bug fixes, and informal adjustments that occur outside formal project structures.

Both types of change are considered normal and expected features of any work system's evolution.

---

## 5. The Work System Method (WSM)

The **Work System Method** is the practical application of WST. It is a flexible systems analysis and design method that allows business and IS/IT professionals to describe, analyze, design, implement, and evaluate IT-enabled systems. Key uses include:

- Producing management briefings about system improvements.
- Supporting requirements analysis before moving to technical specifications.
- Facilitating communication between business and IS/IT professionals by providing a shared vocabulary and structure.

Over 700 management briefings were produced using WSM by MBA and EMBA students applying the method to real-world systems across various organizations.

## 6. Work System Axioms

Building on the framework, Alter (2025) proposed **24 work system axioms** — statements assumed to be true for every work system within the relevant domain. These axioms are organized into five categories:

| Category | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **System in Context** | Describes how a work system relates to its environment (open system, purposeful design, stakeholders, externalities). |
| **System Operation** | Describes internal functioning (activities, resources, regulation, interactions, maintenance, subsystems). |
| **Goal Attainment** | Addresses how and why a system achieves — or fails to achieve — its goals (goal variety, alignment, trade-offs, operational fit, adaptability). |
| **Operational Variability** | Recognizes real-world uncertainty (human and automated agency, compliance and workarounds, performance uncertainty). |
| **System Change** | Covers how systems evolve (design incompletion, planned and unplanned change, path dependence, absorptive capacity). |

These axioms are intended to be universally applicable to all work systems (and hence all information systems) within the defined domain, as opposed to theories that apply only to some instances.

---

## 7. Scope and Boundaries

The WSF is most relevant for:
- **Typical operational work systems** — neither tiny nor huge.
- **Systems designed to operate over weeks, months, or years**.
- Both **sociotechnical and totally automated** systems.

The WSF does *not* cover:
- Natural systems (biological, geological, etc.).
- Static representational systems (software code, ontologies as abstract structures).
- Very brief personal activities (e.g., playing a phone game).
- Huge enterprise-wide transformations (too large to be analyzed as a single system).

---

## 8. Why WSF Matters

The WSF addresses a persistent challenge in organizations: **business and IS/IT professionals often struggle to understand and communicate about systems**. By offering a structured, non-technical vocabulary covering both the human and technical dimensions of work systems, the framework:

- Helps surface issues that might otherwise be taken for granted.
- Supports productive collaboration between people with different expertise.
- Provides a starting point for requirements analysis before technical specifications are written.
- Can be augmented with AI tools (e.g., LLMs using WST as a knowledge base) to support early stages of systems analysis.
